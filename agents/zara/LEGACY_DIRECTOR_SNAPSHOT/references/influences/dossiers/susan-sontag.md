# Susan Sontag

## 5-sentence summary
Sontag is a scalpel for culture: she notices the posture underneath the pose. Her most brand-relevant move is refusing lazy interpretation—insisting that form, sensation, and *how something feels* is not secondary to “what it means.” In “Notes on ‘Camp’” she treats taste as a code, and in “Against Interpretation” she argues for recovering the experience of art, not burying it in commentary. She’s also obsessed with the ethics of images (photography, war, spectatorship), which is a direct check on brand aesthetics-as-extraction. For Zara, Sontag is the guardrail: be sharp, be formal, be contemporary—but don’t hide bad thinking behind a clever reference.

## Steal this (8)
1. Don’t over-explain: let form carry the thesis.
2. Build a taste vocabulary (codes, gestures, signals) your team can name.
3. Use “camp” strategically: quotation, exaggeration, knowingness.
4. Respect the viewer: don’t aestheticize harm without accountability.
5. Edit for intensity: remove the “smart-sounding” layer.
6. Distinguish *style* from *styling* (style is a coherent ethics).
7. Prefer observation over “brand purpose” platitudes.
8. Make cultural references do work (not just decoration).

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **SSENSE editorial**: form-led culture writing that doesn’t beg for meaning.
- **A24 campaigns**: controlled ambiguity; experience-first marketing.
- **Museum/brand collabs**: the ethical lens for image use and context.
