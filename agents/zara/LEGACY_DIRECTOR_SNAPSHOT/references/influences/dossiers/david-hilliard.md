# David Hilliard

## 5-sentence summary
Hilliard’s signature is the triptych: intimate, autobiographical scenes expanded into panoramas that feel both staged and true. He uses serial panels to show time shifts, emotional contradictions, and the social texture around a subject—especially family, sexuality, and small-town life. The effect is documentary honesty with constructed composition: a narrative you can read left-to-right. For brands, Hilliard is a blueprint for telling richer stories without video—sequence creates depth. For Zara, he’s the reference for intimacy with structure: personal, vulnerable imagery that still feels designed.

## Steal this (8)
1. Use multi-panel storytelling (diptych/triptych) to add time and nuance.
2. Compose with emotional progression, not symmetry.
3. Keep color natural but intentional (skin, light, domestic tones).
4. Let environment disclose character.
5. Mix candid moments with subtle staging.
6. Use repetition with variation to build meaning.
7. Caption sparingly; let sequence do work.
8. Make vulnerability feel safe, not exploitative.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Brand storytelling carousels**: 3-frame narratives designed for scroll.
- **Editorial product stories**: product as part of life, not center stage.
- **Community portraits**: serial format to show complexity and trust.
