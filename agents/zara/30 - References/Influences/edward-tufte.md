# Edward Tufte

## 5-sentence summary
Tufte is the patron of high-signal information: dense, truthful, and respectful of the reader’s intelligence. He argues against chartjunk and for data-ink efficiency, small multiples, and evidence-rich displays that let comparisons emerge. His best lesson for brands is that clarity is not simplification; it’s structured complexity presented cleanly. He’s also a moralist about integrity—bad charts are lies with typography. For Zara, Tufte is how you make sophistication feel calm: pack meaning into the page, then remove everything that isn’t meaning.

## Steal this (8)
1. Maximize signal-to-noise; delete decoration first.
2. Use small multiples for comparisons (systems beat hero charts).
3. Put context next to claims (sources, baselines, units).
4. Design for reading, not scanning—let depth exist.
5. Prefer tables when they’re the honest tool.
6. Use annotation as narrative: captions that teach.
7. Build an “integrity checklist” for visuals.
8. Make hierarchy from spacing and alignment, not loud styling.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Product analytics dashboards**: small multiples + consistent scales.
- **Investor/strategy decks**: evidence-first pages with tight annotation.
- **News graphics**: layered context without visual shouting.
