# Carl Jung

## 5-sentence summary
Jung is the deep-structure guy: psyche as a landscape of recurring patterns (archetypes) plus personal shadow material you’d rather not see. He’s useful less as “mystical vibes” and more as a toolkit for narrative consistency—why certain images, roles, and conflicts keep working across cultures. The brand takeaway: people don’t just buy features; they buy *roles* (Explorer, Caregiver, Trickster) and *transformations* (before/after). Jung also warns you: what you repress shows up anyway, so brands need to integrate their contradictions instead of airbrushing them. For Zara, Jung is a lens to make an identity system emotionally legible without being literal or cheesy.

## Steal this (8)
1. Build brand worlds around an archetypal *role* (not a slogan).
2. Give the audience a “transformation promise” (initiation, mastery, liberation).
3. Name the shadow: what the brand refuses to be (and how it leaks).
4. Use symbols, not illustrations: let motifs carry meaning across touchpoints.
5. Make rituals: onboarding, purchase, unboxing, renewal as repeatable ceremonies.
6. Keep polarity: order/chaos, restraint/excess—tension creates energy.
7. Prefer mythic structure (quest, descent, return) for campaigns.
8. Don’t force universals; localize archetypes through culture and context.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Nike / athlete-as-hero**: consistent myth (trial → mastery) across products and stories.
- **Headspace / Calm**: ritualized daily practice + symbolic calm world.
- **Skincare brands**: transformation narrative done with restraint (avoid “miracle,” show journey).

## Image / visual keywords (3)

- shadow / chiaroscuro

## Visual signature (3-5 references)
- The Red Book (Liber Novus) — Intricate, mythic, and hand-painted symbolic illustrations.
- Mandala Drawings — Concentric, geometric symbols of psychic wholeness.
- Bollingen Tower — Jung's hand-built stone retreat, full of carved inscriptions and symbols.
