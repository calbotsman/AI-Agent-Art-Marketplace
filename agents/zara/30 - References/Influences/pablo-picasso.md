# Pablo Picasso

## 5-sentence summary
Picasso is the engine of reinvention: relentless iteration, style shifts, and the conviction that form can be rebuilt from first principles. Cubism is the most brand-relevant chapter—showing how to depict reality by decomposing it into viewpoints and reassembling a new truth. The real lesson isn’t “make it weird”; it’s that a strong concept can survive radical formal change. Picasso also demonstrates the power (and risk) of omnivorous appropriation. For Zara, Picasso is permission to prototype identities fast—while keeping a central idea stable.

## Steal this (8)
1. Prototype aggressively: many versions beat one perfect attempt.
2. Separate concept from execution so you can evolve without losing meaning.
3. Use fragmentation to reveal structure (what’s essential?).
4. Build series: identity as an evolving body of work.
5. Borrow widely, but acknowledge lineage and context.
6. Use reduction + exaggeration to clarify.
7. Let contradictions coexist (beauty/violence, play/terror).
8. Treat “style changes” as strategic pivots, not inconsistency.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Seasonal brand systems**: one core idea, multiple formal skins.
- **Product families**: consistent function with varied expressions.
- **Campaign art direction**: multiple viewpoints to tell a single story.
