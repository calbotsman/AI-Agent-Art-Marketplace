# Yves Saint Laurent (YSL)

## 5-sentence summary
YSL is sharp elegance with an edge: tailoring, black-as-architecture, and a brand that made modern sexuality legible through clothes. The house built iconic codes (Le Smoking, tuxedo language, sharp shoulders) that are instantly readable and endlessly remixable. Under later eras (including Saint Laurent branding), the lesson becomes how to modernize without losing codes—tight typography, stark imagery, and a controlled palette. YSL also proves that “minimal” can be aggressive, not soft. For Zara, YSL is the template for iconic code discipline: pick a few elements and make them unstoppable.

## Steal this (8)
1. Build signature codes (shape + palette + attitude) and repeat them.
2. Use black/white as a system, not a default.
3. Let typography carry authority (tight, controlled, high-contrast).
4. Make imagery feel like a stance (not a mood).
5. Design for recognizability at distance.
6. Modernize by tightening rules, not by adding novelty.
7. Keep sensuality sharp—avoid softness unless intentional.
8. Make hero products into myths (names, stories, rituals).

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Luxury fragrance**: code-driven packaging + stark campaigns.
- **High-end fashion e-comm**: typographic authority + aggressive whitespace.
- **Premium nightlife/hospitality**: black-as-brand with tailored details.

## Visual signature (3-5 references)
- Le Smoking (1966) — The first tuxedo for women; sharp tailoring as liberation.
- Mondrian Dress (1965) — Direct translation of abstract art into fashion form.
- Saharienne (Safari Jacket) — Transforming utilitarian/military gear into high-fashion elegance.
