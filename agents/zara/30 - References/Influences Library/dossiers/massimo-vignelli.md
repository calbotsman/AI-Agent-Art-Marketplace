# Massimo Vignelli

## 5-sentence summary
Vignelli is grid-as-morality: a belief that clarity comes from disciplined structure, limited typographic palettes, and repeatable rules. His work spans corporate identity, signage, furniture, and the famous (controversial) 1972 NYC Subway map—an emblem of prioritizing system over literal representation. The key is his insistence on a small set of “right” tools (typefaces, grids) used with mastery. He can read authoritarian if imitated blindly; the trick is to steal the discipline, not the dogma. For Zara, Vignelli is a forcing function: fewer choices, higher quality decisions.

## Steal this (8)
1. Limit the palette (typefaces, weights, colors) to increase coherence.
2. Use grids as a production tool, not a retro style.
3. Prefer hierarchy over decoration.
4. Make wayfinding thinking transferable to product navigation.
5. Design for reproduction and consistency across vendors.
6. Treat typography as architecture (spacing, rhythm, scale).
7. If you abstract reality, be explicit about the benefit (speed, clarity).
8. Build manuals that a team can follow.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Transit/wayfinding systems**: clarity under pressure.
- **Design systems**: small token sets with strict rules.
- **Premium retail packaging**: restraint + typographic authority.
