# Henri Matisse

## 5-sentence summary
Matisse is color used as structure: not filling shapes, but building space and emotion with flat planes and decisive contours. He’s a master of simplification—complex scenes reduced to confident, legible forms that still feel luxurious. Late cut-outs prove an important brand lesson: constraints (age, tools) can produce a new language that’s more direct than “refined” painting. Matisse is also about *pleasure* without kitsch—sensuality handled with control. For Zara, Matisse is how you make bold feel elegant: fewer shapes, stronger color decisions.

## Steal this (8)
1. Simplify until the silhouette reads instantly.
2. Use color to create hierarchy (not just contrast).
3. Keep line quality intentional—one confident contour beats texture.
4. Balance decorative energy with structural composition.
5. Use cut-out thinking: assemble from large, clear pieces.
6. Let negative space be active.
7. Don’t fear ornament; just discipline it.
8. Build “joy” as a design goal—measurable in lightness and clarity.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Premium wellness brands**: confident color + calm composition.
- **Editorial illustration systems**: cut-out style with disciplined palettes.
- **Fashion prints**: large shapes, limited colors, high legibility.

## Image / visual keywords (3)

## Visual signature (3-5 references)
- 'The Snail' (1953) — Bold, abstract gouache cut-outs exploring pure color relationships.
- 'Icarus' from Jazz (1947) — Simple, mythic silhouette against a vibrant blue field.
- 'The Red Studio' (1911) — Radical use of flat, saturated color to define architectural space.
