# Peter Bürger — *Theory of the Avant-Garde*

## 5-sentence summary
Bürger’s thesis: the “historical avant-garde” (Dada, Surrealism, etc.) didn’t just invent new styles; it attacked the *institution of art*—trying to fuse art back into life. When the avant-garde becomes a museum category, its shock turns into a repeatable look, and “radical” becomes décor. That’s brutal and incredibly useful for brand work: it explains why subversion so quickly becomes a template. Bürger is not a hype-man; he’s a diagnostic tool for when “experimental” is actually just aesthetic innovation with no institutional stakes. For Zara, this is the anti-cosplay filter: if you borrow avant-garde language, you need a real operational change (distribution, authorship, participation), not just posters.

## Steal this (8)
1. Don’t confuse new form with new function (ask: what institution changes?).
2. If you use “anti-” positioning, make it visible in operations.
3. Build participation/agency into the work (not just audience consumption).
4. Treat “shock” as a diminishing asset; design for longevity.
5. Make critique legible without footnotes—clarity is part of the attack.
6. Prefer interventions in systems (pricing, access, ownership) over styling.
7. Beware commodified rebellion; it reads instantly now.
8. Keep a “recuperation plan”: how your idea gets absorbed, and what you do next.

## Concrete modern applications (3)
- **Patagonia**: institutional choices (repair, activism) that match the rhetoric.
- **Open-source brands/tools**: shifting authorship and participation.
- **Creator-led product drops**: when done with genuine revenue/credit structures.
